Most Digital Workers Get This Completely Wrong

They think the solution is blocking blue light. The real solution is reducing visual workload.

1

They Think Blue Light Is the Main Problem

Blue light blocking is the most marketed solution for screen strain. So people assume it's the most important. But accommodative fatigue—the exhaustion your ciliary muscle feels from constant near-focus—accounts for far more discomfort than blue light ever could. Filtering light without addressing workload is like treating a headache with a cold compress when the real issue is dehydration.

2

They Assume Clear Vision Means Healthy Eyes

If you can read text clearly, your eyes must be fine, right? Wrong. Your visual system compensates for small errors to keep things sharp. But compensation costs energy. After hours of screen work, that energy runs out—and you feel it as tightness, pressure, and pain. Clarity and comfort are not the same thing. And most people don't realize that until someone measures the difference.

3

They Buy Generic Glasses Without Knowing Their Exact Measurements

Store-bought blue light glasses are made with an average pupillary distance (usually 63mm). But if yours is 60mm or 66mm, light is entering your eyes at the wrong angle. Your brain corrects for it. Your eyes strain to compensate. And by the end of the day, you're exhausted—not because the glasses are "bad," but because they were never designed for your eyes.
